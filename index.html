The Argument
In a world of screens, boards still matter.

Long before the glow of a smartphone screen or the flicker of a television set, human beings gathered around tables to play. They moved carved pieces across grids, challenged each other with words, bargained for territory, and built entire social rituals around the act of sitting down together and competing.

Today, we live in an era saturated with digital entertainment. Streaming services, video games, and social media compete ferociously for every idle moment of our attention. And yet, board game sales continue to climb year after year. Cafes dedicate entire walls to shelves of boxed games. Families rediscover the kitchen table.

Board games offer something no algorithm can replicate: the irreplaceable texture of shared physical space. The weight of a chess piece in your hand. The rustling of Scrabble tiles. The theater of passing Monopoly money. These tactile, embodied experiences anchor us to one another in ways that a multiplayer lobby simply cannot.

There is strategy, yes ‚Äî but there is also laughter, tension, negotiation, and story. Every game is a narrative written in real time, remembered long after the pieces are packed away.
