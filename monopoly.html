History

A Game Born from Protest

Monopoly's origins are more radical than its corporate reputation suggests. In 1903, progressive activist Elizabeth Magie invented the Landlord's Game as a political teaching tool ‚Äî a deliberate critique of land monopolism and the exploitative economics championed by industrialists. She wanted players to feel, firsthand, how landlords enrich themselves while tenants grow poor. The game had two rule sets: one rewarding monopoly, one rewarding shared wealth. Only one set survived history.

Across the following decades, the game spread through folk culture, adapted and re-themed by communities across the United States ‚Äî Quaker college towns, Depression-era families, Atlantic City summer vacationers who gave the board its iconic street names. In 1935, Charles Darrow brought his version to Parker Brothers, who marketed it as the all-American success story. They patented it. Magie received a flat payment of 500 dollars and no royalties.

Despite ‚Äî or perhaps because of ‚Äî this contested origin, Monopoly became one of the best-selling board games in history. Over a billion people across more than 100 countries have played it. It has been released in hundreds of editions, used in wartime to smuggle escape tools to prisoners of war, and debated endlessly as both game and cultural mirror.

How to Play

The Rules of the Real Estate Empire

Players move tokens around the board by rolling two dice, buying properties they land on, and charging rent to opponents who land on their holdings. The goal is simple and brutal: be the last player with money when everyone else goes bankrupt.

  • Each player starts with $1,500 in Monopoly money divided across various denominations.
  • On your turn, roll both dice and advance your token by the total. Doubles let you roll again.
  • Land on an unowned property? Buy it at face value or auction it to the highest bidder.
  • Land on someone else's property? Pay rent ‚Äî which rises steeply with houses and hotels.
  • Collect a color group and you can build houses (then hotels) to dramatically multiply rent.
  • Community Chest and Chance cards introduce randomness: windfalls, penalties, and instructions.
  • Roll three doubles in a row and go directly to Jail. Pay $50, use a Get Out of Jail Free card, or roll doubles to escape.
  • A player who cannot pay a debt is bankrupt and eliminated. Last one standing wins.
